Electricity

Electric Circuit and Current

‚ñº
Electric Circuit: A continuous closed path of an electric current.
Electric Current: The rate of flow of electric charge through any cross-section of a conductor in unit time.

Direction of current is taken as opposite to the direction of flow of electrons.

Ammeter: The instrument used to measure electric current. It is always connected in series in an electrical circuit.
1 Ampere: If 1 coulomb of charge flows through any cross-section of a conductor in 1 second, then current is 1 ampere.

Electric Potential and Potential Difference

‚ñº
Electric Potential: It is defined as the amount of work done to move a charge from one point to another point.
Voltmeter: The instrument used to measure potential difference. It is always connected in parallel across the resistor in an electrical circuit.
1 Volt: If 1 joule of work is done in moving 1 coulomb of charge, then the potential difference between the points is 1 volt.

Resistance and Conductors

‚ñº
Resistance (R): A constant for a given metallic wire at a given temperature. It is the property of a conductor to resist the flow of charges through it. Its SI unit is ohm, represented by the Greek letter Œ©.
Variable resistance: A component used to regulate current without changing the voltage source.
Rheostat: A device used to change the resistance in a circuit.

S.I unit of resistance is 'Ohm' (Œ©).

1 Ohm: If a potential difference of 1 volt across the ends of a conductor makes a current 1 ampere flow, then the resistance of the conductor is 1 ohm.

The resistance of a conductor depends on the following factors:

  • R is directly proportional to the length of the conductor (l).
  • R is inversely proportional to the area of cross-section (A).
  • Nature of the material.
  • Temperature.
Good conductor: A component of a given size that offers a low resistance.
Resistor: A conductor having some appreciable resistance.
Poor conductor: A component of identical size that offers a higher resistance.
Insulator: Offers even higher resistance than poor conductors of the same size.
Resistivity of alloys: Generally higher than that of constituent metals.
Applications of materials: Alloys are used in electrical heating devices; tungsten for filaments of electric bulbs; copper and aluminum for electrical transmission lines.

Series and Parallel Connections

‚ñº
Series Connection: In series connection, potential difference across each resistance is different but the current through each resistance is the same.
Parallel Connection: In parallel connection the potential difference across each resistance is the same but the current through each resistance is different.
All electrical appliances in houses are connected in parallel, as different objects operate at different currents.

Electric Power

‚ñº
Electric Power: It is defined as the amount of electric energy consumed in a circuit per unit time.
Watt (W): The SI unit of electric power. It is the power consumed by a device that carries 1 A of current when operated at a potential difference of 1 V.
1 Watt: Electric power is said to be 1 watt, if 1 ampere current flows through a circuit having 1 volt potential difference.

S.I unit of heat developed is Joule (J).

Magnetic Effects of Electric Current

Magnets and Poles

‚ñº
North Pole: The end pointing towards north is called north seeking or north pole.
South Pole: The other end that points towards south is called south seeking or south pole.
Like poles repel, while unlike poles of magnets attract each other.

Magnetic Field

‚ñº
Magnetic Field: Exists in the region surrounding a magnet, in which the force of the magnet can be detected.
Field Lines: Used to represent a magnetic field. A field line is the path along which a hypothetical free north pole would tend to move.
A current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force. When current and magnetic field are perpendicular, the force is maximum and perpendicular to both.

Electromagnets and Solenoids

‚ñº
Solenoid: A coil of many circular turns of insulated copper wire wrapped closely in the shape of a cylinder.
Electromagnet: A coil of many circular turns of insulated copper wire wrapped around a soft iron core.

Electrical Safety

‚ñº
Live Wire: Usually has red insulation, carries current at 220V potential (in India).
Neutral Wire: Has black insulation.
Earth Wire: Has green insulation, connected to a metal plate in the earth.
Electric Fuse: Safety device that prevents damage due to overloading or short-circuiting.
Short Circuit: Occurs when live and neutral wires come in direct contact.

Light, Reflection and Refraction

Reflection of Light

‚ñº
Reflection of Light: Bouncing back of light after it falls on a reflecting surface or a polished surface such as a mirror.
Image formed by plane mirror: A plane mirror forms a virtual, erect image. The size of the image is equal to the size of the object. The image is laterally inverted.

Spherical Mirrors

‚ñº
Spherical Mirrors: Mirrors whose reflecting surfaces are curved are called spherical mirrors.
Concave Mirror: Its reflecting surface is curved inside (inwards).
Convex Mirror: Its reflecting surface is curved outwards.
Pole: The centre of the reflecting surface of a spherical mirror is a point called its pole. It lies on the surface. It is represented by 'P'.
Centre of Curvature: The reflecting surface of a spherical mirror forms a part of a sphere. The centre of the sphere is called the centre of curvature. It is represented by 'C'.
Principal Axis: The imaginary line that passes through the centre of curvature and the pole of the mirror is called principal axis.
Principal Focus: The rays which are parallel to the principal axis after reflection from the surface of a convex mirror or after reflection from the surface of a concave mirror appear to have come from a point on its principal axis. This point is called principal focus of the convex mirror or the principal focus of the concave mirror. This point is called 'f'.
Radius of Curvature: The distance between pole of the mirror and Centre of curvature. It is represented by 'R'.
Focal Length: The distance between pole of the mirror and Principal Focus. It is represented by (f).
Aperture: The diameter of reflecting surface of a spherical mirror is called aperture.

Lenses

‚ñº
Spherical Lens: A transparent material bounded by two surfaces of which one or both surface are spherical is called a spherical lens.
Convex Lens: Both the surfaces are bulged outwards. Thick in the middle and thin at the edges. It is a converging lens.
Concave Lens: Both the surfaces are curved inwards. Thick at the edges and thin in the middle. It is a diverging lens.
Principal Axis: An imaginary straight line passing through the two centres of curvature of a lens is called its principal axis.
Optical Centre: The central point of a lens is its optical centre.
Aperture: The effective diameter of the circular outline of a spherical lens is called its aperture.
Thin Lenses: Lenses whose aperture is much less than its radius of curvature and the two centres of curvatures are equidistant from the optical centre O. Such lenses are called thin lenses with small apertures.
Rays of light parallel to the principal axis are falling on a convex lens. These rays, after refraction from the lens, are converging to a point on the principal axis. This point on the principal axis is called the principal focus of the lens.
Rays of light parallel to the principal axis are falling on a concave lens. These rays, after refraction from the lens, are appearing to diverge from a point on the principal axis. This point on the principal axis is called the principal focus of the concave lens.
The degree of convergence or divergence of light rays achieved by a lens is expressed in terms of its power. The power of a lens is defined as the reciprocal of its focal length.

Human Eye and Colorful World

Structure of Human Eye

‚ñº
Human Eye: The human eye is like a camera: Its lens system forms an image on a light-sensitive screen called the retina.
Cornea: Light enters the eye through a thin membrane called the cornea. It forms the transparent bulge on the front surface of the eyeball.
Eyeball: The eyeball is approximately spherical in shape with a diameter of about 2.3 cm.
Crystalline Lens: The crystalline lens merely provides the finer adjustment of focal length required to focus objects at different distances on the retina.
Iris: Iris is a dark muscular diaphragm that controls the size of the pupil.
Pupil: The pupil regulates and controls the amount of light.
Eye Lens: The eye lens forms an inverted real image of the object on the retina.
Retina: The retina is a delicate membrane having enormous number of light-sensitive cells.
Light-sensitive Cells: The light-sensitive cells get activated upon illumination and generate electrical signals.
Optic Nerves: These signals are sent to the brain via the optic nerves. The brain interprets these signals, and finally, processes the information so that we perceive objects as they are.

Vision and Accommodation

‚ñº
Eye Lens Composition: The eye lens is composed of a fibrous, jelly-like material. Its curvature can be modified to some extent by the ciliary muscles.
Power of Accommodation: The ability of the eye lens to adjust its focal length is called accommodation.
Least Distance of Distinct Vision: The minimum distance at which objects can be seen most distinctly without strain. It is also called the near point of the eye.
Near Point: For a young adult with normal vision, the near point is about 25 cm.
Far Point: The farthest point upto which the eye can see objects clearly. It is infinity for a normal eye.
Cataract: Sometimes, the crystalline lens of people at old age becomes milky and cloudy. This condition is called cataract. This causes partial or complete loss of vision.

Light Dispersion and Spectrum

‚ñº
Angle of Prism: The angle between two lateral surfaces of prism.
Spectrum: The band of the coloured components of a light beam is called its spectrum.
Dispersion: The split of light into its component colours is called dispersion.
VIBGYOR: The acronym VIBGYOR will help you to remember the sequence of colours (Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange and Red).
White Light: Any light that gives a spectrum similar to that of sunlight is often referred to as white light. It is caused by dispersion of sunlight by tiny water droplets, present in the atmosphere. A rainbow is always formed in a direction opposite to that of the Sun.

Scattering of Light

‚ñº
Tyndall Effect: The phenomenon of scattering of light by the colloidal particles gives rise to Tyndall effect.
Scattering of Light: When a beam of light strikes such fine particles, the path of the beam becomes visible. The light reaches us, after being reflected diffusely by these particles.
Blue Sky Explanation: The molecules of air and other fine particles in the atmosphere have size smaller than the wavelength of visible light. These are more effective in scattering light of shorter wavelengths at the blue end than light of longer wavelengths at the red end.
Danger Signal Lights: The red is least scattered by fog or smoke. Therefore, it can be seen in the same colour.
